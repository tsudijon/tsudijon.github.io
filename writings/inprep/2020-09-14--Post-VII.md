# Introduction

**Goal**: Define Brownian Motion on manifolds. In particular, consider the *Eells-Elworthy-Malliavin* approach to Brownian motion on manifolds.

What's the ultimate goal of this line of research? Stochastic analysis on manifolds yields a more refined way to analyze geometric objects and to do analysis on manifolds. Some results in the book by Hsu show how relatively well-known geometric theorems can be established probabilistic methods. A common motif in these results seems to be the connection between stochastic processes on manifolds and curvature.

There are several different ways to characterize Brownian Motion on manifolds.

- Extrinsic: via the Nash Embedding theorem, and the Laplace Beltrami operator
- Local: via the heat equation on local coordinate patches.
- Intrinsic: via the *Eells-Elworthy-Malliavin* construction

## Basic Idea of the Intrinsic Approach

Basic idea: one can consider the *Laplace Beltrami* operator on a Riemannian manifold, and use this as the infinitesimal generator of a Markov process, which is exactly the Brownian motion on the manifold. 

From a technical sense, one can appeal to existence results of stochastic differential equations to show the above Brownian motion exists, but the results that were shown in Hsu's book need the Laplace Beltrami operator to be expressible as a sum of squares. As a general note, this can only be done if the manifold can be isometrically embedded into Euclidean space.

What can be done however, is to lift up the equation to the orthonormal frame bundle $\mathscr{O}(M)$ of the manifold. One can then consider Bochner's *Horizontal Laplacian* $\Delta_{\mathscr{O}(M)} = \sum_{i=1}^n H_i^2$ (which is then expressible as a sum of squares). The importance of this object is given by the fact that it relates really nicely to the Laplace Beltrami operator $\Delta_M,$ via the following lifting property:

$$ \Delta_M f(x) = \Delta_{\mathscr{O}(M)}(f \circ \pi)(u)$$,

whenever $\pi(u) = x.$

One can then solve an appropriate SDE on the orthonormal frame bundle, and project the solution down via the lifting property. This is the *Eells-Elworthy-Malliavin* approach to Brownian motion on manifolds.
